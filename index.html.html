RAMONES

1-2-3-4

Ramones. The surprise saviors of rock and roll. From just the name Ramones, you can hear Dee Dee's brash scream in your head, "1-2-3-4!" then a tidal wave of electric guitars like chainsaws roaring in the hands of a masked man running towards you from the dark of the night, the famed wall-of-sound of Tommy's drums so fast the crowd felt like they had to sprint to keep up, and Joey's faux-British, precise, dynamic voice tearing through the jet engine roar like lightning, white hot, the sound of a band on fire. Forest Hills, Queens, New York: ground-zero of the powder keg explosion culminated from the combined pressure of disco on the radio and self-indulgent arena rock taking the music away from its rebellious, underdog, anti-hero roots to elite, extravagant virtuosos forgetting their audience four minutes into the drum solo. The Ramones were amateurish musicians, but genius in their songwriting, schtick, and most importantly delivery of their brand of naked, screaming, campy, and dangerous rock.

Members

Taking their name from an anecdote they heard about Paul McCartney using the pseudoname Paul Ramon in hotels, the Ramones found their name. Early on they wanted the world to think they were brothers, so they each took on a charactature of themselves using Ramone as the common surname. Originally, Dee Dee was going to sing and Joey was going to drum, but Dee Dee's throat got sore after a couple of songs. Tommy was going to be their manager, but Joey had the voice and he couldn't drum and sing at the same time and none of the drummers the auditioned couldn't keep up with the 1/8 notes on the high-hat like Tommy would demonstrate. Johnny was always going to be Johnny. After getting a couple cheap Mosrite guitars at a pawn shop, giving Dee Dee the bass, and Tommy on drums until they found their permanent drummer, their friend Marc Bell in Richard Hell's band the Voidoids, the lineup of the band the would set rock on fire was set.

Not listed is Elvis Ramone. Clem Burke of Blondie sat in with the Ramones after Marky got kicked out. He came up with the iconic name, but in my book, he didn't make the cut. Richie ended up replacing Marky, then he was shown the door when the band wanted Marky back. C.J. replaced Dee Dee in 1989, if memory serves, after Dee Dee suddenly announced he was quitting Ramones to pursue a career as a rapper, rebranding himself Dee Dee King. Fortunately, that was short lived. Even more fortunate was that although he never rejoined the Ramones as a member, he never quit writing songs for them.
